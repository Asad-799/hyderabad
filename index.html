Hyderabad-The city of pearls

Charminar is a historic monument located in Hyderabad, Telangana, India, built in 1591 by Sultan Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah, the fifth ruler of the Qutb Shahi dynasty. It was constructed to commemorate the end of a deadly plague that had afflicted the city and also to mark the founding of Hyderabad as the new capital. The name "Charminar" means "Four Minarets," referring to its four grand towers, each about 56 meters tall, featuring spiral staircases with 149 steps leading to the top, offering panoramic views of the old city. The structure exemplifies Indo-Islamic architecture with Persian influences, built from limestone, granite, and mortar. It is square-shaped with four large arches facing four roads, symbolizing the center of the city. The upper floor houses a mosque that remains in use for prayers, making it one of Hyderabad’s oldest mosques. Surrounding Charminar is the bustling Laad Bazaar, famous for bangles, pearls, perfumes, and traditional attire, making the area vibrant, especially during festivals like Ramzan and Diwali. The monument also has historical significance, with a secret tunnel connecting it to Golconda Fort, believed to serve as an escape route for the Sultan. Charminar is a protected heritage site under the Archaeological Survey of India and is considered a symbol of Hyderabad’s rich culture, history, and architectural excellence. It continues to attract tourists worldwide and stands as a proud emblem of India’s historical legacy.
